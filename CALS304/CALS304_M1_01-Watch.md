# Follow Energy’s Evolution Across Human History

Imagine the world of our ancestors seven million years ago — roughly
350,000 generations into the past.

The planet was warmer than today, though beginning a long cooling trend
that would eventually usher in the first great ice ages. This era is
sometimes called the “planet of the apes,” because large primates
occupied much of the landscape. Among them was *Australopithecus* — one
of the first to walk upright. You may have heard of Lucy, the so-called
“missing link.” She was an Australopithecus.

These were not humans in the way we would recognize ourselves. But they
were upright. They used tools. And like every organism before them, they
lived entirely within the limits of energy.

Energy is the capacity to do work — to move something, heat something,
transform something. Without energy, nothing happens. In physics, we
measure energy in **joules**. Think of a joule as a tiny jelly bean
sitting in a jar. One jelly bean does nothing on its own. But if you eat
it — if your body converts its sugars into heat, motion, or thought —
energy is released.

One joule isn’t much. But civilization runs on unfathomable piles of
them.

An average adult male today requires roughly 2,500–3,000 food Calories
per day. Each food Calorie (kilocalorie) contains approximately 4,184
joules. That means sustaining one human body requires on the order of
ten million joules per day.

For most of evolutionary history, that metabolic flow was the entire
energy budget of our ancestors. As Vaclav Smil (2017) explains, early
human energetics were almost entirely metabolic, constrained by the
efficiency of food acquisition and digestion.

There were no engines. No fuels. No electricity. Energy scarcity was
biological.

At some point more than a million years ago, upright, tool-using,
language-capable hominids learned to control fire (Smil, 2017). This
marked humanity’s first major external energy breakthrough. Fire allowed
cooking, which increased caloric availability; it extended habitable
territory and reshaped landscapes. For the first time in Earth’s
history, a species deliberately managed combustion.

Even so, energy remained constrained by contemporary solar flows. Wood
came from nearby ecosystems. Total energy use was small and local.

With agriculture, humans reorganized ecosystems to capture solar energy
more deliberately. Cropland and pasture replaced forests. Animals
converted plant energy into mechanical work. Biomass supplied heat. Over
the past 10,000 years, global land use shifted dramatically toward
agricultural systems (Ritchie & Roser, 2019). Growth depended on land
because land captured sunlight through photosynthesis.

For millennia, civilizations operated within this solar ceiling (Smil,
2017).

Then fossil fuels broke that constraint.

Coal, oil, and natural gas are ancient sunlight stored over millions of
years. Burning them allowed societies to access energy flows vastly
greater than annual photosynthesis. Per capita energy use rose from tens
of megajoules per day in foraging societies to hundreds or thousands in
industrial economies (Smil, 2017).

In just a few centuries, humanity shifted from living within annual
solar income to drawing down solar savings accumulated across deep time.

That shift altered the structure of civilization itself. When energy is
scarce, societies organize around constraint. When energy becomes
abundant, institutions expand and expectations rise.

Every civilization is, at its core, an energy system.

And every energy system carries its own constraints.
