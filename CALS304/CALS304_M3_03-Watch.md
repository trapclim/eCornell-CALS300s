In New York State, the climate action plans are very ambitious, with the goal of becoming completely free of fossil fuels by the year 2040.

A central and essential component of that plan includes what they call dispatchable emissions-free resources, or DEFRs.

I do not know if that acronym is meant to be ironic, because they have essentially deferred the details of what those resources would look like to some future date.

But these resources are a requirement for an emissions-free grid.

Right now, it is conventional hydrocarbons that are able to supply reliable power when wind or solar are insufficient to meet demand.

We are fortunate that we also have nuclear power plants and large-scale hydro facilities, including the one in Quebec from which we import a great deal of electricity.

Even so, we are still going to need more of these DEFRs.

Something on the order of 20 to 40 gigawatts of DEFR capacity is going to be needed to keep the grid stable and prevent blackouts.

Those blackouts could be catastrophic, especially in the winter.

A great deal of thought and research is going into this, because think about what this could look like in practice.

We scale up solar and scale up wind, temperatures keep getting hotter, and wildfire risks keep increasing this decade and next.

Then, suddenly, the grid fails because it has become too reliant on renewables without adequate backup.

What is going to happen then?

We will take a giant step backward and start using, or keep using, fossil fuels for longer than intended, perhaps well past 2040.

New York State is also a great case study in how electricity is actually produced, priced, and consumed.

In New York State, the New York Independent System Operator, or NYISO, is responsible for ensuring that the supply and demand for electricity are always exactly balanced.

This balance is critical. If too much electricity is produced and not consumed, systems can overload and fail, and if too little is produced, the result is blackouts, which can be dangerous, especially in winter.

To manage this, NYISO continuously balances how much electricity is being produced by sources like natural gas, nuclear, hydropower, solar, and wind with how much is expected to be consumed.

They rely on forecasting, including weather predictions, and operate markets on very short time scales, ranging from minutes to days, to coordinate this process.

Electricity is bought and sold in these markets at constantly changing prices.

For example, a nuclear plant might have relatively high production costs but can generate power consistently, while a solar farm may produce cheaper electricity but only during daylight hours.

As a result, electricity on the grid typically comes from a mix of sources, each contributing at different times and price points.

Managing that mix manually would be extremely complex, but most consumers do not have to do this themselves.

Instead, utilities like NYSEG, the New York State Electric and Gas Company, handle this process.

NYSEG owns and maintains much of the infrastructure used to move electricity, including power lines, and is responsible for delivering electricity to homes and businesses.

They purchase electricity through NYISO at fluctuating market prices but sell it to customers at relatively stable rates.

In this way, they absorb the volatility in energy markets and provide consistency for consumers.

This system contrasts with what has happened in places like Texas.

In 2021, during an extreme cold event, parts of the Texas grid experienced widespread failures.

The system there relied heavily on natural gas, which struggled under freezing conditions, and the grid had limited ability to import electricity from other regions.

At the same time, electricity markets allowed prices to fluctuate freely based on supply and demand.

As power generation capacity dropped and demand surged, prices increased dramatically.

In some cases, consumers faced extremely high electricity bills because they were exposed directly to wholesale market prices.

This highlights the importance of both grid stability and market design.

Electricity is unusual compared to most commodities because it must be consumed almost immediately after it is produced.

There is very limited large-scale storage, which means supply and demand must be balanced in real time.

Consider a fish markeet.

Fish must be sold and consumed quickly, and supply must closely match demand to avoid waste or shortages.

Electricity operates in a similar way, but on even shorter time scales.

In this analogy, NYISO functions like the market operator, ensuring that enough “supply” is brought in to meet demand without excess or shortage.

Utilities like NYSEG act more like distributors or intermediaries, purchasing energy from the market and delivering it to customers at stable prices.

In systems without that buffering role, like parts of Texas during the 2021 crisis, consumers may be directly exposed to price volatility and supply disruptions.

Understanding these dynamics is essential as we think about the future of energy systems.

Balancing supply and demand, managing variability, and designing markets that are both efficient and resilient will all be critical challenges moving forward.
