# Module 4 Final Project

## Designing Integrated Strategies to Scale Renewable Energy Deployment

## Introduction

Energy transitions are structural transformations. They alter
infrastructure, industrial supply chains, labor systems, and material
flows. They unfold over decades because they require the replacement of
long-lived capital assets and embedded industrial ecosystems (Smil,
2010).

Modern civilization operates on historically unprecedented levels of
energy and material throughput (Smil, 2017). Renewable expansion must
therefore confront not only emissions goals, but the physical scale of
steel, cement, transmission, mining, and manufacturing systems that
support contemporary economies.

This project asks you to design a strategy that acknowledges those
constraints while pursuing rapid decarbonization.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Scenario

You are advising a regional planning coalition committed to expanding
renewable electricity.

There is agreement on the goal. There is disagreement on the pace.

One group prioritizes speed to reduce cumulative emissions. Another
emphasizes durability---supply chain resilience, grid stability, and
workforce capacity.

Your task is to design an integrated strategy that:

-   Identifies binding constraints\
-   Sequences interventions across time horizons\
-   Connects regional policy to global supply systems\
-   Balances urgency with structural realism

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Project Structure (Canvas New Quiz Format)

### Part I --- Structural Foundations

**Question 1 (Multiple Choice)**\
Which factor most consistently explains why energy transitions unfold
over decades?

A. Insufficient public support\
B. Low technology performance\
C. Infrastructure turnover and capital stock inertia\
D. Seasonal weather variability

**Correct Answer: C**

Large-scale systems change slowly because infrastructure replacement
requires sustained capital investment and institutional coordination
(Smil, 2010; Smil, 2021).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Part II --- Regional Constraint Diagnosis

Identify your region and describe:

-   Its renewable ambition\
-   Its most binding deployment constraint\
-   Why that constraint binds within its broader industrial system

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Part III --- Industrial and Supply Chain Integration

Select a primary renewable pathway for your region.

1.  Identify two critical material or manufacturing constraints.\
2.  Explain how those constraints connect to global supply chains.\
3.  Propose one realistic mitigation strategy for each.

Regional strategies operate within global material systems shaped by
extraction, refining, and manufacturing capacity (Smil, 2017).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Part IV --- Sequencing Under Constraint

Compare:

-   A speed-prioritized deployment strategy\
-   A durability-prioritized deployment strategy

Discuss trade-offs and propose a sequencing approach that balances
emissions reduction with system stability.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Part V --- Three-Horizon Strategy

Develop a roadmap:

**Immediate (0--3 Years)**\
Relieve immediate bottlenecks and improve interconnection efficiency.

**Medium-Term (3--10 Years)**\
Expand manufacturing capacity and workforce pipelines.

**Long-Term (10--25 Years)**\
Address capital stock turnover and industrial decarbonization.

Each action must specify: - The constraint addressed\
- Responsible actors\
- Expected system outcomes
