## Project Page — Examining the Evolution of Energy Systems and Their Impact on Modern Consumption

Modern consumption is not an accident. It is the outcome of energy systems that made high rates of production, transport, and control routine. When energy becomes denser, cheaper, more portable, and more reliably converted into useful work, societies reorganize around that capacity. What changes first is infrastructure. What changes next is labor, mobility, and material expectations. What changes last is what people consider “normal” (Smil, 2017).

Smil’s historical point is straightforward: premodern societies were constrained by low-power energy conversions. Wood has relatively low energy density, and open fires and simple hearths convert it inefficiently. The combined result was limited mechanical power, limited heat at scale, and persistent patterns in daily life (Smil, 2020). Transitions break inertia, but they do not do so overnight. They are system shifts—resource extraction, conversion devices, distribution networks, and end uses must all expand together (Smil, 2017).

In this project, you will analyze one primary energy source as a system, not a fuel. You will (1) summarize how reliance on that source changed over time, (2) link those changes to modern consumption patterns, and (3) evaluate a plausible 2035–2050 constraint scenario using **energy density**, **EROI**, and **infrastructure lock-in**.

# Canvas “New Quiz” structure

## Question 1 (Multiple Choice): What does EROI describe?
**Prompt:** Which statement best describes EROI?  
A. The dollar cost of producing a unit of energy  
B. The ratio of energy gained to energy spent to obtain that energy  
C. The fraction of energy that becomes electricity in a power plant  
D. The amount of energy stored in a fuel per kilogram

**Correct answer:** B  
**Feedback if correct:** Correct. EROI is a net-energy measure: energy returned relative to energy invested.  
**Feedback if incorrect:** EROI compares **energy output to energy input** for obtaining usable energy—not price or conversion efficiency.
