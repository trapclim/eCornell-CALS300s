### Introduction and scenario
Energy systems change slowly. A major accident can halt investment for years. A brief global slowdown can produce a visible emissions dip—and then emissions rebound as the system returns to its usual routines. That pattern is not a verdict against change. It is a reminder that energy is a large, interconnected set of technologies, infrastructures, markets, and rules. It does not respond quickly to single choices made in isolation.

Vaclav Smil’s basic point is useful here: energy transitions are not gadget swaps. They are system rebuilds—fuels, prime movers, infrastructures, and institutions moving together. Because the built world is heavy, transitions are usually measured in decades (Smil, 2010; Smil, 2024). citeturn2view0turn0search10

That historical pace matters because the present task is time-bound. The IPCC concludes that pathways limiting warming to 1.5°C or 2°C involve **rapid and deep** greenhouse-gas emission reductions across sectors, alongside a shift from fossil fuels without CCS toward very low- or zero-carbon energy sources (IPCC, 2022). citeturn4view2

In this project, you are an **energy analyst** advising a decision-maker (a government ministry, a city, an NGO, or a company board) that wants to learn from the past while planning for a **low-carbon transition** today. You will select **one historical energy transition** and use it as a lens: what drove the shift, what enabled it, what followed as consequences, and what became difficult to change later. Then you’ll compare that story to the present moment—where the challenge is not only delivering energy services, but also reducing greenhouse-gas emissions on a tight timeline.

# Quiz Questions (New Quiz)

## Question 1 (Multiple Choice | 4 points)  
**Prompt:** In the historical transition you chose, which factor most directly explains why a new fuel or energy carrier displaced an older dominant source?

A. The new system always reduced environmental harm  
B. The new system delivered energy services more cheaply, reliably, or at larger scale given available technology and institutions  
C. The old system had no remaining resources  
D. The transition occurred mainly because people preferred novelty

**Correct answer:** B

**Feedback (Correct):**  
Good. Transitions typically spread when a new system offers more useful services per cost, per labor hour, or per unit infrastructure—given the social and political machinery that can support it. Environmental outcomes may improve or worsen, but they are rarely the main driver at the outset.

**Feedback (Incorrect):**  
Recheck the core logic of historical transitions. Resource depletion can matter, but it is not usually the single trigger; “preference” is not an explanation; and environmental improvement is often incidental or delayed. Look instead for changes in **cost, reliability, scale, and fit with institutions and infrastructure**.

## Question 2 (Short Answer | 6 points)  
**Prompt:** What “problem” did your chosen transition solve at the time?  
In 150–250 words, describe the **energy-service constraint** that made the old system feel inadequate (e.g., industrial heat, mobility, urban lighting, manufacturing throughput, land constraints, labor constraints). Name at least **one** concept from the module (energy density, EROI, or efficiency) that helps explain why the new system looked attractive.

**Optional evidence anchor (Smil):** If it fits your case, you may use representative energy-density values often cited by Smil: air-dry wood ~17 MJ/kg; bituminous coal ~22–25 MJ/kg; refined oil products ~42 MJ/kg. citeturn2view0

**Scoring guide (for instructors):**
- Clear description of the historical constraint/problem (2)  
- Accurate application of at least one module concept (3)  
- Specificity and coherence (1)

**Feedback (General—shown after submission):**  
Strong answers name a concrete constraint (“factories needed concentrated heat,” “cities needed dependable lighting,” “transport demanded portable energy”) and connect it to why the new system had leverage—often through **higher energy density**, favorable **EROI**, or improved **efficiency** in converting fuel into useful work.

## Question 3 (Essay | 12 points)  
**Prompt:** Explain what enabled the transition *and* what it locked in.  
Write 600–900 words addressing all four components below:

1) **Enablers:** What technologies, infrastructures, policies, capital flows, or resource endowments made the transition possible?  
2) **Unintended consequences:** What social, environmental, or geopolitical effects followed that were not the primary “goal”?  
3) **Lock-in/path dependence:** What investments or systems became hard to reverse (transport networks, industrial layouts, supply chains, political interests, consumer habits)?  
4) **Course concepts:** Use **at least two** of the following accurately: energy density, EROI, efficiency, infrastructure inertia/lock-in.

**Scoring guide (for instructors):**
- Enablers explained with specificity (3)  
- Unintended consequences thoughtfully analyzed (3)  
- Lock-in described clearly and plausibly (3)  
- Correct and meaningful use of ≥2 course concepts (3)

**Feedback (General—shown after submission):**  
The most persuasive essays treat infrastructure as durable commitment: once built, it favors the fuel and technology it was designed to use. Lock-in is not only technical; it is also institutional and cultural. Use material details—pipelines, ports, grids, engines, financing, labor, laws—and explain how they constrain later change.

## Question 4 (Comparative Recommendation Memo | 8 points)  
**Prompt:** Now compare your historical case to today’s fossil-to-clean transition and recommend an action.  
Write a brief memo (350–550 words) to your chosen stakeholder that answers:

- **Similarities:** One way today’s transition resembles your historical case  
- **Differences/constraints:** Two ways today is fundamentally harder (choose from: climate deadlines, existing fossil infrastructure, global inequality, political coordination, supply-chain constraints, intermittency/storage, land/mineral limits, incumbency power)  
- **Recommendation:** One specific, realistic recommendation the stakeholder should act on in the next 1–3 years, justified using your comparison

**Scoring guide (for instructors):**
- One credible similarity (2)  
- Two credible, well-explained differences (4)  
- Actionable recommendation tied to evidence and concepts (2)

**Feedback (General—shown after submission):**  
A good memo avoids slogans. It treats “clean energy” as a system that must be built and operated—supply chains, siting and permitting, finance, and grid capacity—under a clock that past transitions did not face. The recommendation should be concrete and feasible within 1–3 years.
