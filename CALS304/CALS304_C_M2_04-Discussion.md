# Identifying Nuclear Energy’s Role in Addressing Climate Change (Discussion)

An energy source can look “clean” in one spreadsheet and feel anything but clean on the ground. Uranium can power a reactor with low direct carbon emissions, yet the costs of mining and contamination have sometimes landed hardest on communities with the least power to refuse them. Cases like uranium extraction on Navajo lands make a simple point that’s easy to lose in climate debates: *where* harms fall matters as much as *how much* harm shows up in national averages.

At the same time, the climate problem has a stubborn scale. Nuclear power draws strong opinions because it sits where urgency meets unease—steady electricity and low operational emissions on one side; waste, cost, accident risk (however rare), and public trust on the other. Smil’s histories of energy are useful here: societies do not “switch fuels” in isolation. Energy choices shape institutions, infrastructure, and daily life, and transitions unfold through systems that resist quick, tidy change (Smil, 2017; Smil, 2020).

One more complication: the global record looks different depending on what you count. Using a deaths-per-electricity metric that combines accident fatalities and air-pollution impacts, Our World in Data reports nuclear at roughly **0.04 deaths per terawatt-hour**, in the same low range as wind and solar, and far below fossil fuels (Ritchie, Rosado, & Roser, 2019). That kind of statistic is informative—but it does not cancel questions about waste, governance, or the distribution of risk. It does, however, clarify why the debate persists: nuclear can be framed as both a safety problem and a safety advantage, depending on the boundary of the analysis.

This discussion asks you to evaluate nuclear energy’s role in climate action through three stakeholder lenses. Choose the position you find most defensible (or propose a clearly explained hybrid), then write a post that shows how evidence and values combine in real-world energy decisions.

Choose one stakeholder position to argue from:

- **Phase Out Nuclear NOW!** Despite nuclear’s carbon advantages, long-term waste, accident potential, and “hidden” harms outweigh the benefits. Renewables and storage are scaling rapidly, and continued reliance on nuclear risks locking society into an expensive, centralized, politically fragile system.  
- **Maintain, But Don’t Expand.** Existing nuclear plants provide valuable low-carbon electricity, and closing them too fast can backfire by increasing fossil generation. But building many new plants is slow, costly, and difficult to permit—so nuclear is a bridge for stability, while most new growth should come from renewables.  
- **Expand Nuclear Investment.** Climate urgency requires every scalable low-carbon tool. Nuclear’s reliability and energy density matter, and newer designs plus stronger oversight could reduce risks. With appropriate governance, nuclear can help meet rising demand while cutting emissions.

Structure your post around the following prompt:

- **What is your chosen pathway, and what is the strongest reason for it?**  
- **What key tradeoff does your position accept** (cost, waste, speed, reliability, accident risk, land use, or public trust)—and why is it worth it?  
- **How does your stance respond to legacy harms and uneven burdens?** Name at least one concrete safeguard or policy you would insist on to prevent repeating localized damage.  
- **What assumption does your argument rely on most** (deployment speed, cost, regulatory feasibility, grid needs, technology improvement, or social acceptance)—and where could that assumption fail?  
- **If you had to steelman one opposing position, what would you say?** Then explain why you still disagree.

As you write, keep one additional consideration in mind: even the best argument has to operate inside real systems—permitting, markets, infrastructure lock-in, and shocks that can swing public sentiment quickly. These forces do not settle the debate, but they shape what becomes feasible (Smil, 2017).
