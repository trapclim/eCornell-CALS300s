## Energy Systems and Everyday Life

Differences in food emissions illustrate a broader principle: energy use
and emissions scale together. Systems that rely heavily on fossil fuels
generate corresponding carbon dioxide outputs.

This relationship extends beyond agriculture.

Modern societies operate through large and continuous energy
flows---transport networks, electric grids, heating systems,
manufacturing, and digital infrastructure. Fossil fuels remain central
to these systems (Smil, 2017). The scale of modern energy use
distinguishes contemporary civilization from all previous energy
regimes.

Understanding global emissions begins with examining how energy moves
through daily life.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Transportation

Transportation is often the largest contributor to individual emissions
in high-income countries (Ritchie & Roser, 2023).

Approximate emissions factors:

  Activity                                            CO₂ Emissions (approx.)
  ----------------------------------- ---------------------------------------
  Gasoline vehicle                                      \~0.4 kg CO₂ per mile
  Domestic flight                       \~0.2--0.25 kg CO₂ per passenger-mile
  Public transit                                   \~0.1--0.2 kg CO₂ per mile
  Electric vehicle (U.S. grid avg.)                \~0.1--0.2 kg CO₂ per mile

Driving 12,000 miles annually in a gasoline vehicle results in roughly
**4,800 kg CO₂**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Electricity and Heating

Electricity and thermal energy are foundational to modern life.

  Energy Source                CO₂ Emissions (approx.)
  -------------------------- -------------------------
  U.S. average electricity        \~0.4 kg CO₂ per kWh
  Natural gas heating           \~5.3 kg CO₂ per therm
  Heating oil                   \~10 kg CO₂ per gallon

A household consuming 10,800 kWh annually produces approximately **4,300
kg CO₂**, depending on grid composition.

Traditional agrarian societies operated at roughly **20--30 gigajoules
per capita annually**, primarily from biomass, compared to over **300
gigajoules per capita** in many modern high-income societies (Smil,
2017).

## Diet and Indirect Consumption

Food-related emissions vary widely, particularly between plant-based and
animal-based products (Poore & Nemecek, 2018; Ritchie & Roser, 2023).

Beyond diet, indirect energy use includes:

-   Manufactured goods\
-   Shipping and logistics\
-   Digital infrastructure\
-   Cooling and air conditioning

These systems are integrated and energy-intensive.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Estimating Personal Emissions

Exact accounting is not necessary to identify scale.

Begin with approximations:

1.  Annual vehicle mileage\
2.  Annual electricity consumption\
3.  Flight frequency\
4.  Heating fuel usage\
5.  General dietary pattern

Reference values:

  Category                         Annual Activity   Emissions Estimate
  ------------------------------ ----------------- --------------------
  Driving                             10,000 miles       \~4,000 kg CO₂
  Electricity                           10,800 kWh       \~4,300 kg CO₂
  Domestic flight (round-trip)       \~2,000 miles    \~400--500 kg CO₂
  High beef diet                               ---      \~1,500+ kg CO₂

Global comparison:

-   Global average: \~4--5 metric tons CO₂ per person per year\
-   United States average: \~14--15 metric tons (Ritchie & Roser, 2023)

Many individuals in high-income countries fall between **8 and 20 metric
tons annually**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Illustrative Annual Profile

Example:

-   12,000 miles driving → \~4,800 kg CO₂\
-   11,000 kWh electricity → \~4,400 kg CO₂\
-   2 domestic flights → \~900 kg CO₂\
-   Moderate meat consumption → \~1,000 kg CO₂

Estimated total: **\~11 metric tons CO₂ per year**.

The distribution across categories is often more informative than the
total.

## Historical Comparison

Pre-industrial societies emitted relatively little carbon dioxide
because they consumed little fossil energy (Smil, 2017). Lower
throughput limited mechanized transport, industrial production, and
large-scale heating.

Modern systems provide mobility, climate control, refrigeration, and
digital connectivity. These benefits are tied to higher energy
consumption and higher emissions.

Energy surplus expands capability.

It also increases atmospheric burden.
