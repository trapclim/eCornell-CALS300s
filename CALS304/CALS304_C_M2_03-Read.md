# Evaluate the Costs of Localized Harm in Energy Systems

Numbers can steady the mind. When we compare energy sources, we often reach for the cleanest-looking metrics: deaths per unit of electricity, lifecycle carbon intensity, even the “carbon debt” of building the thing in the first place. Our World in Data’s comparisons, for instance, make it easy to see why fossil fuels—especially coal—carry heavy human and environmental costs, and why nuclear can appear, on certain measures, surprisingly low in both emissions and fatalities (Ritchie & Roser, 2017; Our World in Data, n.d.-a). Those charts are useful precisely because they discipline the conversation; they pull it back from pure narrative and into evidence.

But evidence can be incomplete in a very particular way. Energy systems do not rest on the planet evenly. The benefits can radiate outward, while the risks and residues settle into specific places—often where the power to refuse is thin. This page looks at what gets blurred when we speak only in averages, by turning to one place where nuclear’s “clean” reputation begins much earlier in the chain: uranium mining on Navajo lands.

## Global Metrics and Local Realities

Big-picture metrics are designed to see across systems, not into them. A single value—deaths per terawatt-hour, grams of CO₂-equivalent per kilowatt-hour—compresses a long sequence of extraction, processing, transport, and disposal into something comparable. That compression is practical, but it can hide distribution: who faces the daily hazards, who lives near the waste, who inherits the cleanup (Ritchie & Roser, 2017; Our World in Data, n.d.-a).

Václav Smil offers a helpful way to think about this problem of scale. He notes that earlier environmental harms were often local or regional—serious, but bounded—while modern transitions have produced deterioration that is widespread and even global, without eliminating the concentrated burdens that gather at points of extraction and material throughput (Smil, 2020). In other words, the harm can be both everywhere and somewhere—and the “somewhere” matters.

## Uranium on Navajo Lands: A Case in Concentrated Burden (1944–1986)

Between 1944 and 1986, uranium deposits across Navajo territory were intensively mined under federal priorities tied first to wartime weapons development and then to Cold War demand. The work was dangerous in ways that were not always communicated clearly or effectively to the people doing it. In many operations, ventilation and protective measures lagged behind what would have been needed to reduce exposure, and miners inhaled radioactive dust over long periods.

The consequences did not arrive all at once. They appeared years later—through elevated rates of lung cancer and other radiation-linked illnesses—when the mines were quiet and the contracts long closed. And the boundary between mine and home was porous. Dust clung to clothing and tools, traveling back into households, where family members could be exposed without ever entering a shaft.

Long after mining slowed, its residues remained. Abandoned mines and tailings became enduring features of the landscape, complicating water use, land safety, and cleanup. This is one of the defining features of localized harm: the timeline stretches beyond the economic moment that caused it.

## Cultural and Social Costs Beyond the Ledger

For the Navajo, land and water are not merely inputs to be managed; they are bound up with identity, memory, and ceremony. When extraction contaminates a place, the damage is not only chemical. It can be relational—altering what feels safe, what feels sacred, what feels possible.

These harms are also shaped by access to information and power. When technical risks are poorly explained, when language barriers and unequal institutions leave communities outside decision-making, the result is not merely misunderstanding. It is dispossession by procedure: decisions made “for” people who will live with the consequences.

## What “Clean” Must Include

The point of this case is not to replace one slogan with another. It is to widen the frame. Nuclear power may score well on certain global indicators, and OWID-style comparisons are a useful reminder that fossil fuels impose enormous harms through both air pollution and carbon emissions (Ritchie & Roser, 2017; Our World in Data, n.d.-a). Lifecycle comparisons also reinforce that low-carbon electricity options—including nuclear—can have low greenhouse-gas emissions per kilowatt-hour when assessed across supply chains rather than only at the smokestack (Our World in Data, n.d.-b).

But a low average does not automatically mean a fair system. A pathway can be low-carbon and still be unjust if it concentrates risk and residue in places that are already politically and economically vulnerable.

When weighing any energy option, it helps to keep a second set of questions beside the first:

- Where do the harms occur—especially in routine extraction and processing, not only in rare accidents?  
- Who bears them, and who benefits?  
- How long do the harms last, and who is responsible when the original project ends?  
- Were communities informed, able to consent, and able to refuse?

These questions do not weaken the climate imperative. They sharpen it. They insist that solving one crisis should not require quietly exporting another.

## Closing Synthesis

The charts and datasets that compare safety and “greenness” are necessary tools, and they often point toward sensible conclusions about what to avoid and what to consider (Ritchie & Roser, 2017; Our World in Data, n.d.-a). Still, the moral ledger is longer than the axis labels. Smil’s reminder that modern transitions generate both widespread impacts and concentrated burdens is a reason to look closely at where the burdens fall (Smil, 2020). The Navajo uranium legacy shows how electricity that looks “clean” downstream can carry dirty consequences at its source—and how any serious evaluation of nuclear’s role has to hold both truths at once.
