# Re-Examine the Consensus to Understand the Boundaries of Climate Reports

When you finished your last project, I hope you felt some of the tension most of us who do climate science feel with some regularity.

You start with science you know is rock-solid and robust, and yet, when you try to apply those global findings to a specific location, the uncertainties blur the picture and force you to use more circumspect language.

It forces you to hedge.

It forces you to try to err on the side of caution, but then realize you do not quite know what caution means in this context. Should you plan for the worst-case scenario? The most likely bad outcome? The median? The mean?

As I told you at the end of the last module, welcome to the club.

IPCC reports are excellent starting points for evaluating exposure to climate risk. However, by their very nature, they are always at least a little behind cutting-edge science, maybe a year or two behind, not to mention behind the pace of current warming itself.

And their insistence on consensus and uncertainty quantification, while scientifically satisfying, can de-emphasize some of the more alarming findings.

Thousands of studies sit at the base of every assessment. These include regional drought analyses, papers on tipping points, high-impact low-probability risks, new modeling approaches, novel statistical techniques, bold claims that grab headlines, and articles refuting some of those claims.

The authors read all of those papers, and they ask: Do multiple lines of evidence support a given claim? Do different methodological approaches converge on similar conclusions? Is there agreement on a topic across the relevant peer-reviewed literature?

If the answer is yes — for example, if the question is, Are humans causing global warming? — then that finding gets elevated and featured, maybe as high up as the Summary for Policymakers.

But when the answer is, It depends — which is more often the case — the claim does not disappear. It gets qualified and couched in the appropriate scientific context.

That framing protects the scientific credibility of the reports.

It ensures that central conclusions can withstand scrutiny from hundreds of scientists and, in the case of the Summary for Policymakers, delegates from countries throughout the world.

You saw earlier how the Summary for Policymakers gets approved line by line. That means leading scientists and political delegates, including delegates from fossil-fuel-producing countries, have to agree, line by line, on every sentence.

So what you end up with is a scientifically and geopolitically acceptable compromise.

But now we have to talk about what gets left out.

New areas of research, sometimes the most relevant for what could happen in the coming decades, often only show up in a handful of studies. Some tipping point dynamics, such as the loss of the Greenland Ice Sheet or major Arctic ice changes, remain plausible but not fully constrained. Others may be impossible to constrain for now, but also impossible to rule out.

For example, how likely is it that the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation will shut down? That would have a devastating effect on the climate of Western Europe. This would be an extremely high-magnitude event if it happened.

But we do not know how to quantify the likelihood of it occurring.

Yet that probability is certainly greater than zero.

You almost have to read the reports as if they were written in a different language. They distinguish very likely from likely, then define what those words mean in terms of probabilities somewhere else in the report. They use terms like high confidence and medium confidence without always making it obvious, in the moment, what confidence means unless you go looking for the definition elsewhere in the document.

The problem, and I can say this because I am not one of the authors, is this:

It is an exquisite report written by scientists and researchers, yet its language does not always convey the seriousness of the science.

It does not, and by its nature cannot, fully tell you why you should care if the Atlantic circulation shuts down. It also cannot fully convey the seriousness of regional risks or local climate impacts.

This is where people often misunderstand climate assessments. They assume that cautious language means weak science.

That is not actually true.

The irony is that the cautious language often means the science is being done very well and communicated accurately by scientists.

This is the essence of the IPCC tension, and I think the sad irony at the heart of the process.

By trying to construct an unassailable scientific document, the process also produces something that can be difficult to access unless you already know how to read it.

If you include too much speculative or nuanced material, or too many projections of events that do not materialize quickly, then the report risks losing credibility.

But if you leave out too much, or hedge too much, or soften the language too much, then you risk understating the urgency of action.

The next lesson will examine what happens when urgency and uncertainty collide.

I hope you will come away with a better sense of how the scientific discipline applied to these reports, and the language used in them, can both clarify and obscure immediate local realities.

On the one hand, that tension can be frustrating.

On the other hand, I hope you can appreciate what the IPCC is charged with doing.

It is, perhaps, a good kind of tension.

Sometimes tension is a requirement for structural integrity.

If there were no tension in the report, it would not reflect the reality of the enormous amount of high-quality scientific research that sits behind it.
