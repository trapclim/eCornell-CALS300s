## Introduction

In the last segment, we examined the cost of delay.

Carbon accumulates.\
Budgets shrink.\
The longer mitigation is postponed, the steeper the transition becomes.

Now we move from urgency to allocation.

If action matters --- and it does --- where does it matter most?

Mitigation does not happen in theory. It happens inside sectors: energy,
transportation, industry, agriculture, buildings. These sectors are not
abstract categories. They are physical systems --- power plants,
vehicles, furnaces, livestock operations, transmission lines,
insulation, pipelines.

To design effective mitigation, we must first understand where emissions
originate and how those systems connect.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Mapping Emissions: Scale Is Only the Beginning

Globally, emissions cluster in a handful of sectors.

Electricity and heat production account for one of the largest shares.
Transportation and industry follow. Agriculture and land use contribute
differently --- often through biological processes rather than
combustion.

Scale matters.

But leverage is not determined by scale alone.

Electricity occupies a special position because it sits upstream.

Transportation depends on fuel.\
Buildings depend on electricity and heating.\
Industry depends on power inputs.

When electricity becomes low-emission, every sector that electrifies
inherits that reduction.

That is not just mitigation.\
It is multiplication.

This is why Working Group III emphasizes power-sector decarbonization as
a central pathway across scenarios (IPCC, 2022b).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Sectoral Differences in Readiness and Inertia

Not all sectors move at the same speed.

Solar and wind technologies are commercially deployed and expanding
rapidly in many regions. Battery storage continues to improve.
Transmission expansion remains challenging, but technically feasible.

In contrast, cement and steel production rely on chemical processes that
are harder to electrify. Facilities operate for decades. Industrial
transitions require new technologies, infrastructure coordination, and
capital investment.

Transportation falls in between.

Passenger vehicles turn over steadily. Electrification is viable. But
charging infrastructure and grid capacity must scale in parallel.

Agriculture introduces another layer.

Methane from livestock and nitrous oxide from soils arise from
biological systems. Mitigation depends on management practices,
innovation, and in some cases dietary change. The mechanisms differ
fundamentally from replacing a fuel source.

Buildings illustrate yet another pattern.

Individual upgrades --- insulation, heat pumps, efficiency improvements
--- may appear small. But aggregated across millions of structures, they
produce substantial reductions.

So mitigation pathways differ in technical readiness, capital turnover,
infrastructure dependence, and behavioral complexity.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Leverage Through Interdependence

What emerges is a systems problem.

Some sectors reduce emissions directly.\
Others unlock reductions elsewhere.

Decarbonizing electricity enables vehicle electrification.\
Clean power supports low-emission hydrogen for industry.\
Grid modernization shapes building performance.

The highest-leverage interventions often occur where sectors intersect.

Mitigation strategy is therefore not a list of isolated actions. It is a
sequencing problem under constraint.

Where do early investments expand future options?\
Where do infrastructure lifetimes lock in emissions?\
Where does technological maturity allow rapid deployment?

These questions turn mitigation from aspiration into planning.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Regional Context and Structural Differences

Sectoral importance also varies by region.

In rapidly developing economies, infrastructure is still being built.
Decisions made now determine emissions trajectories for decades.

In highly industrialized economies, legacy systems dominate.
Retrofitting becomes central.

Agricultural emissions dominate in some regions. Industrial emissions
dominate in others. Energy access, economic structure, and policy
capacity shape feasible pathways.

There is no single blueprint.

But there is structure.

Working Group III does not present mitigation as one solution. It charts
multiple pathways, each constrained by sectoral realities and system
interdependence (IPCC, 2022b).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Conclusion

Understanding sectoral emissions transforms climate action from
something abstract into something operational.

It allows us to distinguish:

Where reductions are technically mature.\
Where innovation is required.\
Where infrastructure timing matters.\
Where interventions unlock cascading benefits.

In the next segment, we will examine how economists and modelers
formalize these tradeoffs --- how sequencing, cost curves, and system
interactions shape national and global mitigation strategies.

Urgency established the need to act.

Sector mapping reveals where action multiplies.

That is the beginning of strategy.
