# Adapt Global Findings for Regional Stakeholders to Inform Local Actions

If you asked me to go into a grocery store and tell you what kinds of berries they had, and I came back and said, “They have blueberries,” I would not be wrong. But I also would not have given you a complete picture of what was actually in the store.

A more accurate approach would be to send in a large team of people and ask them to document every item in the store that contains berries: fresh berries, dried berries, yogurts, cereals, chocolate bars, anything with berry ingredients. Then that team would compare notes, debate classifications, and produce a long report describing the full range of berry-related products in the store.

That is much closer to what the IPCC does.

A fundamental task of the IPCC is to assess the full body of published climate research relevant to the topics assigned to each chapter during the scoping process. Its role is not to highlight one or two studies. Its role is to evaluate the entire range of evidence. That means reviewing a very large scientific literature, weighing the quality of the evidence, comparing results across methods and datasets, and identifying findings that hold up across multiple independent lines of research.

The result is a careful synthesis. It is valuable precisely because it is broad, rigorous, and international. It identifies robust trends that appear across variables, across datasets, across working groups, and across multiple independent lines of evidence.

But the breadth and scope that make IPCC reports so authoritative also make them difficult to use for local decisions.

Usually, after the IPCC finishes its work and publishes its reports, national climate assessments narrow the focus. They take these global patterns and trends and relate them to the sectors and variables most relevant within a given country: transportation, energy, agriculture, water resources, infrastructure, and public health. They examine how warming translates into regional drought frequency, coastal flooding, wildfire exposure, agricultural losses, and other national-scale risks.

Then some states, including New York, narrow the assessment even further.

These state-level reports combine the global and national literature with climate models, downscaling methods, and local observations to produce higher-resolution information. They focus on variables that are especially relevant to stakeholders in a particular region. That might include the number of very hot days per year, increases in nighttime temperatures, or changes in the frequency of extreme rainfall.

As you move from global to regional to local scales, uncertainties generally increase. There are good reasons for that. It is harder to say exactly when and where certain extremes will occur than it is to identify large-scale warming trends. But it would be incorrect to conclude from this that there is nothing useful we can say, or nothing useful we can do.

Consider a dairy farm in the Northeast.

Heat stress in cattle depends on temperature, humidity, airflow, and overnight cooling. If a report tells you there will be more days above 90 degrees in the future, that is useful in a general sense, but it is not enough for planning. What matters to the farmer is how often those thresholds will be exceeded, how long the heat will persist, how warm the nights will be, how much milk production may be lost, and whether it makes sense to invest in cooling infrastructure now or ten years from now.

That is why state, county, and sometimes city-specific reports matter. Their purpose is to translate international and national consensus reports into the kinds of climate variables and risk metrics that people need in order to make decisions over the coming decades.

The IPCC cannot tell a dairy farmer exactly when to install a new cooling system. It is not supposed to. The IPCC provides the broad scientific foundation. Actionable information comes from combining that robust large-scale foundation with additional, locally relevant information, always with uncertainties clearly qualified.

Doing this well is a balancing act.

You do not want to ignore the robust but relatively coarse information that comes from global assessments. But you also do not want to place too much confidence in highly specific predictions for a single location decades into the future. Anyone trying to sell you precise forecasts for your own backyard far into the future is very likely overstating the current state of the science.

What my group and many of my colleagues try to do with stakeholders is find the useful middle ground. We start by identifying thresholds that matter in practice: days above 90 degrees for dairy cattle, for example. Once those thresholds are identified, we work backward and ask how likely they are to be exceeded under different climate scenarios, given the uncertainties and the range of possible futures.

I saw the same logic in earlier work in the Southwest. There, drought depends on both temperature and precipitation. Precipitation trends can be uncertain, but in many systems, especially during summer, rising temperatures strongly increase evaporative demand and often dominate the drought signal. In that case, even with uncertainty in rainfall, the temperature trend still gives you useful information for planning.

That is the general principle.

You identify the thresholds that matter to the stakeholder. Then you ask what the models, observations, and regional assessments can say about the probability of crossing those thresholds. That is how climate science becomes useful for decision-making.

So the value of global assessments is not that they answer every local question directly. Their value is that they establish the most robust large-scale patterns and mechanisms. National, state, and local assessments then translate that information into forms that are more relevant to specific places, sectors, and decisions.

The science begins globally. But adaptation happens locally. And responsible climate planning depends on knowing how to connect those two scales without overstating certainty at either end.
